If you have applied for a software developer role in Australia recently, you already know: the process has become exhausting.

You polish your resume, tailor your cover letter, then get sent a HackerRank link with a 90-minute timer. After that comes a SHL reasoning test. Then a values-based questionnaire. Then a panel interview with behavioural questions. Then maybe a take-home project. All of this before a single human has looked at your code in context.

For junior developers and recent graduates, this gauntlet is not just stressful. It is often deeply discouraging — and increasingly, it is filtering out capable people for the wrong reasons.

This post covers what is actually happening in Australian tech hiring right now, why so many strong candidates are being screened out, and what you can do if you are in the middle of it.

---

## The State of IT Jobs in Australia in 2026

The Australian tech job market in 2026 is a study in contradictions.

On one hand, demand for digital skills remains high. The [Australian Computer Society's Digital Pulse report](https://www.acs.org.au/insightsandpublications/reports-publications/digital-pulse.html) consistently shows that Australia needs tens of thousands of additional tech workers each year. Federal government digital transformation, health tech, fintech, and e-commerce growth are all real drivers of demand.

On the other hand, **the number of applicants per role has exploded**. Remote work normalised globally distributed hiring. Skilled migration programs brought highly experienced offshore candidates into the local talent pool. And AI-assisted job applications have made it trivially easy to apply to 50 roles in a day — which means employers are now drowning in applications.

The result: companies have responded by layering more filters, more automated screening, and more assessments on top of the hiring process. The practical effect falls hardest on junior developers and graduates.

## What Modern Tech Interviews Actually Look Like

A typical mid-to-large Australian employer hiring a junior developer in 2026 might run a process that looks like this:

### Stage 1: Automated Resume Screening
An ATS (Applicant Tracking System) like Workday, Lever, or Greenhouse scans your resume for keywords before a human reads it. If your resume does not contain the right terms, it never reaches a recruiter.

### Stage 2: Cognitive Ability Test
Tools like **SHL**, **Criteria Corp**, **Revelian (now Criteria)**, or **Arctic Shores** send candidates a 20–40 minute online assessment testing:
- **Numerical reasoning** — reading graphs, percentages, data tables
- **Verbal reasoning** — reading comprehension, logical deduction from text
- **Abstract/spatial reasoning** — pattern recognition with shapes and sequences
- **Attention to detail** — spotting errors in tables or strings of data

These tests are not about coding. They are measuring general cognitive ability. Many are strictly timed with 60–90 seconds per question.

### Stage 3: Technical Assessment
Platforms like **HackerRank**, **Codility**, **LeetCode** (via internal portals), or **CoderPad** present algorithmic challenges. At junior level, common question types include:
- String manipulation and array problems
- Basic data structures (stacks, queues, hashmaps)
- Simple recursion
- SQL queries
- Debugging broken code snippets

### Stage 4: Behavioural / Values Interview
The classic STAR format (Situation, Task, Action, Result). Questions like:
- "Tell me about a time you dealt with a difficult teammate."
- "Describe a situation where you had to learn something quickly under pressure."
- "Give an example of when you disagreed with a decision and what you did."

### Stage 5: Technical Interview or System Design
A live coding session or whiteboard problem, often with a senior developer. For juniors, this might be a small practical task like building a function or reviewing a pull request.

### Stage 6: Take-Home Project
Some companies ask for a 4–8 hour unpaid project — build a small app, a REST API, or a data pipeline.

Not every company runs all six stages. But it is not unusual for a candidate to spend **12–20 hours on a single application** before getting a response.

## Why So Many People Cannot Crack These Assessments

### 1. Cognitive Tests Measure Test-Taking, Not Job Performance

The strongest predictor of success in a cognitive assessment is familiarity with cognitive assessments. A developer with 2 years of production experience who has never done a timed abstract reasoning test will often score lower than a recent graduate who spent two weeks on practice platforms.

Companies like SHL and Criteria Corp claim their tests predict job performance. For certain roles, the research supports this. But for software development specifically — a job that involves documentation, collaboration, debugging, reading Stack Overflow, and iterating — a 40-minute puzzle under pressure is a poor proxy.

### 2. Algorithmic Tests Favour CS Graduates Over Self-Taught Developers

LeetCode-style problems test knowledge of algorithms and data structures that are emphasised in computer science degrees but rarely used in day-to-day web or app development. A self-taught developer who has built real products may never have implemented a depth-first search or balanced a binary tree — not because they lack ability, but because frameworks and libraries abstract this away.

The result is a filter that systematically favours CS graduates over bootcamp alumni, career changers, and self-taught developers — even when those candidates are more capable at building and shipping software.

### 3. Behavioural Questions Are a Skill, Not a Reflection of Character

STAR-format behavioural interviews reward people who have been coached in STAR-format behavioural interviews. Candidates from disadvantaged backgrounds, non-English speaking backgrounds, or who simply have not had the opportunity to be coached, perform worse — not because they have worse values, but because they have less practice at packaging their experiences into a specific narrative format.

### 4. Process Fatigue Is Real

When a candidate is applying for 20–30 roles simultaneously, spending 3 hours on a take-home project for each one is not sustainable. Many strong candidates simply run out of energy partway through a process and do not complete it. Companies interpret this as low interest. In reality, it is a rational response to an unreasonable time commitment.

### 5. Feedback Is Rarely Given

Most companies reject candidates at automated screening stages with no feedback. After 15 hours of assessment work, a candidate receives: "We have decided to proceed with other applicants." With no signal about what to improve, the cycle repeats.

## Is This Fair for Junior Developers and Graduates?

Bluntly: **no, not entirely**.

There are legitimate reasons companies screen candidates — high application volumes, limited recruiter time, and real costs of a bad hire. Those are genuine constraints.

But the current system has a fairness problem at several levels:

**Time poverty**: Junior developers often cannot afford to spend 15 unpaid hours per application. Candidates with financial security, supportive families, or more time can invest more in each application and perform better.

**Coaching inequality**: Students at elite universities get career coaching, mock interviews, and alumni networks. Graduates from less prestigious institutions or non-traditional backgrounds do not.

**Neurodiversity**: Timed abstract reasoning tests under pressure are particularly challenging for people with ADHD, dyslexia, or anxiety — none of which are relevant to their ability to write good code.

**The experience paradox**: Entry-level roles increasingly expect 1–2 years of experience. But how do you get experience without being given your first role?

The Australian Human Rights Commission has published guidance on assessment bias in hiring. If you believe a screening process has discriminated against you on the basis of a protected characteristic, it is worth understanding your rights under the [Fair Work Act](https://www.fairwork.gov.au/) and relevant state discrimination legislation.

## Why IT Jobs in Australia Are Getting Harder to Get in 2026

Several structural factors have converged:

### 1. AI Has Changed the Junior Role
Tasks that junior developers used to learn on — writing boilerplate, simple CRUD endpoints, basic UI components — are now handled by AI tools like GitHub Copilot and Cursor. Companies that previously hired two juniors and a senior now hire one senior developer with AI tools. The entry-level pipeline has shrunk.

### 2. Global Talent Competition
Australian companies can now hire remote developers from lower-cost markets. A startup in Sydney can employ an excellent developer from Eastern Europe or Southeast Asia for a fraction of the local cost. This creates downward pressure on junior Australian roles specifically.

### 3. Post-Boom Correction
The 2021–2022 tech hiring boom — driven by pandemic digital acceleration and cheap capital — led to significant overhiring. The correction in 2023–2024 saw major layoffs globally, including Australian offices of large tech companies. Many experienced developers who were laid off are now competing for roles that would previously have gone to juniors.

### 4. Longer Hiring Cycles
Economic uncertainty has made companies more risk-averse. A hire that would have been approved in two weeks in 2022 might now take three months, go through five interview stages, and require sign-off from senior leadership. Roles that get posted sometimes get frozen or cancelled before an offer is made.

## How This Affects Real People

The impact is not abstract. Here is what the data and lived experience of Australian developers actually shows:

- **Graduate underemployment**: Many CS and IT graduates are working in unrelated roles for 6–18 months after finishing their degree while they continue applying.
- **Mental health**: The combination of rejection, no feedback, and financial pressure contributes to anxiety and burnout. BeyondBlue notes that unemployment and job insecurity are significant mental health risk factors.
- **Skills decay**: When graduates spend 12 months applying rather than coding, the skills they built in their degree start to fade. The gap that employers cite as a reason not to hire them is partly created by the hiring process itself.
- **Career pivots**: A significant number of skilled graduates are leaving the IT job market entirely and moving into adjacent fields — data analysis, IT support, project management — not because they lack technical ability but because the developer hiring funnel is unsustainable.

## What Actually Helps

If you are currently in the job market as a junior developer in Australia, here is what actually moves the needle:

### Build a portfolio with real projects
Not tutorials. Not course projects. Real things: a web app you built to solve a problem you had, a tool you made for a community, a small SaaS with users. Employers who look past the automated screening respond to evidence of initiative. A well-built [portfolio website](/hire-me) is one of the highest-ROI things you can do.

### Practice cognitive tests separately from coding
Sites like [JobTestPrep](https://www.jobtestprep.com.au/) and the free SHL practice portal offer timed practice tests. Familiarity with the format alone can improve scores by 15–20%. Treat it like studying for an exam, not a measure of your intelligence.

### LeetCode for the HackerRank stages
Target the **Easy** and **Medium** tiers. Arrays, strings, hashmaps, and basic recursion cover 80% of junior-level assessments. [NeetCode.io](https://neetcode.io) has a structured roadmap. Aim for 30–40 solved problems before your first assessment — quality of understanding over quantity.

### Apply to smaller companies and agencies
SMEs and digital agencies in Australia often have more human-driven hiring processes — a conversation with the founder or a senior developer, a short practical test, an offer. The six-stage enterprise gauntlet is largely a feature of companies with 200+ employees. Boutique web agencies, regional businesses, and startups are often far more accessible.

### Network locally
Sydney, Melbourne, Brisbane, and even Wollongong have active developer communities. [Meetup.com](https://www.meetup.com) lists local tech events. A recommendation from someone inside a company bypasses most of the automated screening.

### Target regional and government digital roles
State and federal government digital agencies (Services Australia, Digital NSW, Services Victoria) hire junior developers and tend to run more structured, merit-based processes with less algorithmic testing. [APSJobs](https://www.apsjobs.gov.au/) and state government career portals are worth monitoring.

The tech hiring process in Australia in 2026 is not broken beyond repair — but it is under real strain.
